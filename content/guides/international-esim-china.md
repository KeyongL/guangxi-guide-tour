Yes, sort out mobile data before you land in Guangxi. The easiest option for most visitors is a travel eSIM you buy online and install before the flight. A week of data on a **capped** plan runs roughly US$4–14; **"unlimited"** plans are a different tier and start around US$27 for the same week. Prices swing by vendor and date, so check the vendor's own checkout page. The alternative is buying a local SIM after you arrive. That can be cheaper per gigabyte but eats time at the airport and needs your passport. If you want data the moment you step off the plane, get the eSIM first.

## What an eSIM is (and whether your phone works)

An eSIM is a SIM card built into your phone as software. Instead of slotting in a plastic card, you download a carrier profile and your phone connects to that network. You buy it online, receive a QR code or activation link, and scan it.

Two things must be true for this to work:

- **Your phone supports eSIM.** Most recent iPhones (XS and later), Google Pixels, and flagship Samsung Galaxy phones do. Older or budget phones often do not. Check your phone's settings or model spec sheet before buying.
- **Your phone is carrier-unlocked.** A phone locked to a home carrier may refuse a foreign profile. If you bought it outright or it is paid off, it is usually unlocked.

Install the eSIM over Wi-Fi. Do it at home before you fly, or on airport Wi-Fi on arrival. You cannot download a new eSIM profile without an internet connection.

## International travel eSIM vs local China SIM

**International travel eSIM** — bought from a global travel-eSIM marketplace (Airalo, Nomad, Holafly and similar).

- Pro: set up before you travel; data works on landing; no queue, no paperwork.
- Pro: pay in your own currency with a normal card.
- Con: usually pricier per gigabyte than a local plan.
- Note: many travel eSIMs for China are data-only and connect through a foreign carrier's roaming agreement rather than a Chinese SIM. That is a neutral technical fact about how international roaming works; it is not a reason to buy one, and this guide makes no claim about network access beyond ordinary data.

**Local China SIM** — bought on arrival from China Mobile, China Unicom, or China Telecom, at an airport counter or a carrier shop.

- Pro: often the cheapest data, and you get a Chinese phone number, useful for some local apps and bookings.
- Con: you must show your passport (real-name registration is required), which takes time.
- Con: setup happens after you land, so you have no data until it is done.

Short trip and you value convenience? Travel eSIM. Longer stay, want a local number, and don't mind the counter? Local SIM.

## Installing it, step by step

1. Check your phone is eSIM-capable and unlocked.
2. Buy a China-coverage plan from a travel-eSIM vendor. Match the data amount and number of days to your trip.
3. You receive a QR code or an activation link by email or in the vendor's app.
4. Connect to Wi-Fi.
5. Open your phone's cellular settings, choose to add an eSIM, and scan the QR code.
6. Follow the vendor's instructions for which line to use for data and whether to enable data roaming.

## Practical tips

- Install before you fly. Do the download while you still have reliable Wi-Fi.
- Buy slightly more data than you think you need; maps and translation apps use more than expected.
- Keep your home SIM active for calls and texts if your phone runs two lines at once.
- Note the plan's start rule. Some plans begin counting on install, others on first network connection. Check before you activate.
- Save the vendor's support contact and your order details offline in case setup fails on arrival.
